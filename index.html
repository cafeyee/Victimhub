Advice for victims

Nau mai, haere mai

If you have been affected by crime, New Zealand Police is here to support you.

We can help guide you through the process, explain what to expect and connect you with support options available to you.

You have rights as a victim and information is available to help you understand these rights throughout the entire justice process.

This section helps you understand whether victim information may apply to you.

It explains how crime can affect people, types of crime and violence, victims’ rights, and Police’s role.

Use the sections below to find the information that fits what happened and what you may need next.

Who is a victim?

A victim of crime is someone who has been directly affected by a crime. You may still be a victim even if you have not reported what happened to Police.

  • A crime was committed against you.
  • You were physically hurt, or your property was taken or damaged.
  • You are the parent, guardian, or family member of a victim.
  • You may still be a victim if you do not report to Police.

Ways you can be affected by crime

Crime can affect people in different ways. The impact does not need to be visible to be real.

If you need support or want to find services that can help, you can also go to Support for victims.

Physical harm

You were hurt or injured because of what happened.

Emotional impact

What happened affected how you feel, cope, or feel safe.

Financial impact

You lost money, had extra costs, or were affected financially because of what happened.

Property loss or damage

Your home, vehicle, belongings, or other property were taken, damaged, or affected.

Sexual harm

You experienced unwanted sexual behaviour, sexual assault, or another sexual offence.

Family or whānau impact

Someone close to you was affected, or your family or whānau was affected by what happened.

Digital impact

Your identity, accounts, devices, images, or online safety were affected.

Your rights as a victim

Information about how victims can expect to be treated and what information, support, and participation rights may apply.

As a victim of crime, you have legal rights during the justice process. This means you should be treated fairly, kept informed, and supported.

    When you report

    During investigation

The role of Police

Information about what Police can do, what Police cannot do, and who else may be able to help.

Police are usually your first contact in the justice system. Police can help you report what has happened, keep you informed where possible, and connect you to support services.

Receive and record your report

Police record the information you give them so it can be assessed and handled through the right process.

Investigate and gather evidence

Police may speak with people, collect information, and gather evidence where appropriate.

Decide whether charges can be laid

Police assess the evidence and decide whether charges can be laid or whether the case should be referred for prosecution.

Follow the law and victims rights

Police must follow the law, respect victims’ rights, and keep records of decisions and actions.

Keep you informed

Police can give updates where they are able to, while protecting privacy and the investigation.

Refer you to support

Police can connect you with support services that may help with emotional, practical, or specialist needs.

Tell you about VNR

Police can explain whether you may be eligible for the Victim Notification Register.

Issue a Police Safety Order

In some family harm situations, Police can issue a Police Safety Order to help create immediate safety.

Treat you with respect

Police should treat you with respect, courtesy, and fairness, and consider your safety, privacy, and needs.

Guarantee an arrest or prosecution

Police assess each case based on the information and evidence available. Some decisions are made by other parts of the justice system.

Guarantee your case will go to court

Not every report leads to court. Police can explain the process and your options.

Make decisions about sentencing

Sentencing is the court role.

Overview

This section explains what may happen after you contact Police and what you can do next.

It separates the Police process, actions you can take after reporting, and the court and justice process.

After you contact Police

After you contact Police, there are several steps that may happen. The process can vary depending on what happened, the information available, and any safety risks. Police may not be able to complete every step in every case, but this overview shows the main path your report may follow.

  1. 1

    Request

    You contact Police to ask for help or report what happened. This may be by calling 111 or 105, reporting online, or visiting a police station.

  2. 2

    Record

    Police record the information you provide. You may receive a reference number so your report can be found again.

  3. 3

    Assess

    Police review the information to understand what has happened, whether there is immediate risk, and what response may be needed.

  4. 4

    Assign

    Your report may be sent to the team or person best placed to respond. This may depend on the type of incident, location, risk, and available information.

  5. 5

    Action

    Police may contact you, gather more information, collect evidence, speak with other people, or take other action where appropriate.

  6. 6

    Outcome

    The outcome will depend on the circumstances. Police may resolve the matter, recover property, identify a person involved, refer the case for prosecution, or take no further action if there is not enough evidence.

  7. 7

    File

    Police may close or file the case when no further action is available at that time. If new information becomes available, Police may review the case again.
